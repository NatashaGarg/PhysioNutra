Comprehensive Management of Shin Splints (MTSS)

Shin Splints, medically known as Medial Tibial Stress Syndrome (MTSS), is a common overuse injury causing pain along the inner edge of the lower leg. Our specialized physiotherapy programs target the root causes to provide long-term relief for runners and athletes.

What Exactly Are Shin Splints?

Shin splints are characterized by inflammation of the muscles, tendons, and thin layer of tissue (periosteum) surrounding the tibia (shin bone). It is an overuse injury resulting from repetitive stress and micro-trauma to the leg structures, common in activities that involve running or jumping.

Key Causes and Risk Factors:

  • Sudden Increase in Activity: Ramping up running mileage or intensity too quickly.
  • Improper Footwear: Worn-out shoes or those lacking proper arch support.
  • Biomechanical Issues: Flat feet (overpronation) or overly rigid arches that affect shock absorption.
  • Muscle Imbalances: Weakness in the hip or calf muscles.

Differentiating Shin Splints from Other Pain

While shin splints are common, it is crucial to rule out more serious conditions. We perform detailed assessments to distinguish MTSS from:

Differential Diagnosis:

  1. Stress Fracture: Pain is highly localized, intense, and often present at rest.
  2. Chronic Exertional Compartment Syndrome (CECS): A feeling of intense pressure/cramping that stops immediately upon ceasing exercise.
  3. Popliteal Artery Entrapment Syndrome (PAES): Symptoms are often associated with vascular issues.

Our Targeted Physiotherapy Treatment Plan

Effective recovery involves more than just rest. Our plan focuses on correcting the underlying causes:

  • Rest and Activity Modification: Temporarily reducing or switching to low-impact activities (swimming, cycling).
  • Load Management: A progressive return-to-running program with monitored intensity.
  • Strengthening: Targeted exercises for the lower leg, glutes, and core to improve shock absorption.
  • Manual Therapy: Soft tissue mobilization to relieve tension in the calf and tibialis anterior muscles.
  • Orthotic Assessment: Recommending custom or off-the-shelf orthotics to correct foot mechanics if necessary.
