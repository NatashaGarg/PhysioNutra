What is Frozen Shoulder?

Frozen shoulder, medically known as adhesive capsulitis, is a condition characterized by stiffness, pain, and progressive loss of range of motion in the shoulder joint. The shoulder capsule (the connective tissue surrounding the shoulder joint) becomes thick, tight, and inflamed, forming adhesions (scar tissue) that restrict movement.

Unlike other shoulder conditions, frozen shoulder has a unique pattern: it progressively worsens over months (freezing stage), remains severely restricted (frozen stage), and then gradually improves (thawing stage). Without proper treatment, this entire cycle can take 1-3 years, significantly impacting your quality of life.

Key Facts About Frozen Shoulder

  • Affects 2-5% of the general population, most commonly between ages 40-60
  • Women are more likely to develop frozen shoulder than men
  • Diabetes increases risk by 10-20% (occurs in 10-38% of diabetics)
  • Can affect either shoulder, rarely occurs in both simultaneously
  • Early physiotherapy intervention can reduce recovery time by 50-70%

Symptoms & Stages of Frozen Shoulder

Frozen shoulder progresses through three distinct stages, each with unique symptoms and duration. Understanding which stage you're in is crucial for effective treatment planning.

Stage 1: Freezing Stage (2-9 months)

1

Freezing Stage

Duration: 2-9 months

Symptoms:

  • Gradual onset of shoulder pain
  • Pain worsens at night, disrupting sleep
  • Progressive loss of range of motion
  • Pain increases with movement
  • Difficulty reaching overhead or behind back
2

Frozen Stage

Duration: 4-12 months

Symptoms:

  • Pain may decrease but stiffness worsens
  • Severely restricted shoulder movement
  • Unable to lift arm above shoulder level
  • Difficulty with daily activities
  • Shoulder feels "locked" in place
3

Thawing Stage

Duration: 6-24 months

Symptoms:

  • Gradual improvement in range of motion
  • Reduced pain and stiffness
  • Slow return to normal function
  • Movement becomes easier over time
  • May not fully return to pre-condition state without treatment
Important Note: The total duration without treatment can be 1-3 years. However, with proper physiotherapy at PhysioNutra Clinic, most patients experience significant improvement within 3-6 months, regardless of the stage.

Causes & Risk Factors

While the exact cause of frozen shoulder isn't fully understood, researchers have identified several risk factors and conditions that significantly increase your likelihood of developing this condition:

Primary Risk Factors:

  • Diabetes: The strongest risk factor - 10-38% of diabetics develop frozen shoulder (compared to 2-5% in general population). Poor blood sugar control increases risk further.
  • Age & Gender: Most common between 40-60 years old, with women being 70% more likely to develop frozen shoulder than men.
  • Prolonged Immobilization: Keeping your shoulder immobile for extended periods due to surgery, fracture, stroke, or injury creates ideal conditions for adhesions to form.
  • Thyroid Disorders: Both hypothyroidism and hyperthyroidism increase risk by 3-5 times.
  • Cardiovascular Disease: Heart disease patients have a higher incidence of frozen shoulder.
  • Parkinson's Disease: Affects 10-30% of Parkinson's patients due to reduced movement.
  • Previous Shoulder Surgery: Any shoulder surgery increases risk, especially rotator cuff repairs.

Secondary Contributing Factors:

  • Autoimmune conditions (rheumatoid arthritis, lupus)
  • Cervical spine disorders affecting nerve supply
  • Repetitive overhead activities or poor posture
  • Previous frozen shoulder in opposite shoulder (20% chance of occurrence)

  • Arthroscopic Capsular Release: Surgical cutting of tight capsule
