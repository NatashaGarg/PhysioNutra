Quick Answer: What are the stages of frozen shoulder?

Frozen shoulder (adhesive capsulitis) progresses through three stages: Stage 1 — Freezing (0–9 months): pain increases, motion starts reducing; Stage 2 — Frozen (4–12 months): maximum stiffness, pain begins to ease; Stage 3 — Thawing (9–26 months): range of motion gradually returns. Without treatment, recovery takes 1–3 years. With targeted physiotherapy at PhysioNutra Clinic, most patients recover significantly faster. Frozen Shoulder — Common Questions

Expert answers by Dr. Tarun Garg, Senior Physiotherapist at PhysioNutra Clinic

Frozen shoulder (adhesive capsulitis) is a painful condition in which the shoulder capsule thickens and tightens around the joint, progressively restricting movement. It develops gradually without any triggering injury and is most common in people aged 40–60. It passes through three stages — freezing, frozen, and thawing — and can last 1–3 years without treatment. Early physiotherapy significantly reduces pain and recovery duration.
Stage 1 — Freezing (0–9 months): Increasing pain, especially at night, with gradual loss of shoulder motion. Most painful stage. Stage 2 — Frozen (4–12 months): Stiffness at maximum; pain begins to reduce; daily activities severely restricted; joint adhesions are fully formed. Stage 3 — Thawing (9–26 months): Range of motion gradually improves; pain decreases steadily. Physiotherapy during thawing phase accelerates functional recovery. Total natural duration: 12–36 months.
With targeted physiotherapy at PhysioNutra Clinic, most patients achieve significant improvement in 3–9 months, depending on the stage at which treatment begins. Early intervention during the freezing stage typically yields the fastest outcomes. Our protocols include manual joint mobilisation, dry needling, electrotherapy, stretching, and a structured home exercise programme.
Frozen shoulder affects approximately 2–5% of the general population. Risk is highest in women aged 40–60. People with diabetes are up to five times more likely to develop it. Other risk factors include thyroid disorders, previous shoulder injury or surgery, prolonged immobilisation (e.g. after a fracture), cardiovascular disease, and Parkinson's disease. People who have had frozen shoulder on one side have a 5–34% chance of developing it on the other side.
Yes. Over 90% of frozen shoulder cases respond well to conservative physiotherapy.
